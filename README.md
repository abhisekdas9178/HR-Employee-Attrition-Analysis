# 👥 HR Employee Attrition Analysis

# About This Project
As someone with an MBA in HR & Finance, I wanted to bridge my domain
knowledge with data analytics skills. This project analyzes IBM's HR
Employee Attrition dataset to uncover why employees leave organizations
and what HR teams can do about it.

**Business Problem**: The company has a 16.12% attrition rate.
Which factors drive this — and how can HR intervene early

## 🛠️ Tools Used
- Python (Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn)
- Jupyter Notebook

## 📊 Key Findings
| Overall Attrition | 16.12% — 1 in 6 employees leaves |
| Overtime | 30.5% attrition vs 10.4% — nearly 3x higher risk 🚨 |
| Monthly Income | Employees who left earned 30% less on average 🚨 |
| Sales Department | Highest attrition at 20.6% — above company average |
| Job Satisfaction (Low) | 22.8% attrition vs 11.3% at highest satisfaction |
| Promotion Delay | Weak driver — almost no difference found |

## 💡 Business Recommendations

### 1. Control Overtime
Overtime is the single biggest attrition driver. HR should audit
departments with high overtime, introduce compensatory offs, and
flag employees with 3+ consecutive months of overtime for manager check-ins.

### 2. Review Salary Structure
Employees who left earned significantly less. Conduct salary benchmarking
against industry standards, especially for entry and mid-level roles.

### 3. Focus on Sales Department
Sales has the highest attrition at 20.6%. Review incentive structures,
target pressures, and conduct stay interviews with first-year Sales employees.

### 4. Track Job Satisfaction Early
Launch quarterly pulse surveys to catch dissatisfaction before it leads
to resignations. Train managers on recognition and feedback culture.

### 5. Honest Insight — Promotion Delay is Weak
Years since last promotion showed almost no difference between those
who left vs stayed. Not every assumption holds in data — acknowledging
weak signals shows analytical maturity.
